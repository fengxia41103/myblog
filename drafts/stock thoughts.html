Stock

    Let's get serious about stocks. There are two ways of investing: pursuing value, or pursuing trend.

    Rules:

    • Don't mix the two camps — if you buy a stock for its value, don't pay much attention to its day-to-day trend. Instead, read its 8K and 10k.

    Use the NASDAQ earning calendar to find your target.

    Value (by me)

    • How to determine its value on paper?
    • What creates value? I think these conditions must be AND:
    • the product/service are in an industry having growing need.
    • product/service is good quality
    • management makes sound decision

    How to find candidates

    I may have to re-read Benjamin's intelligent investor. All below are yr-to-yr.

    1. Revenue growth above a threshold
    2. Operating income/cash growing. Even better, if it takes a consistent % or larger of the revenue → company is operating to grow, not doing some other tricks such as finance.
    3. Debt-to-income ratio is not blowing up ← a leverage threshold?
    4. No ugly insider. Any of the below should trigger a NO:
    5. executives scandal
    6. big bonus when it's downsizing
    7. execs sell off stocks in large numbers

    DCF model

    Time to pick up your MBA.

    1. Cash + cash equivalent assets.
    2. Current revenue
    3. Future revenue as current rev * (1+growth rate)^year for 1-10 years. 6-10 uses rate 50% of the 1-5's.
    4. Discount future revenues w/ a rate ← use \beta to look up a discount rate.
    5. Total debts, both long & short-term.
    6. Value = (cash + discounted revenues - total debts)/shares

    Trend (by computer)

    Trend is essentially saying that there is an underline current despite of the daily up/down. Well, for a company if I'm identifying its long-term trend as growth, isn't it the same as investing for value? Therefore, I think this thread should not address much as how good it is in the long run, but can be taken blindly only on basis of some calculation from historicals instead of much knowledge of the company.

    • keep going up: buy things that are rising
    • quit at certain gain
    • quit when trend is reversed ← negative direction, and above a threshold.
    • down → up: buy things that just fell, but I believe it will come back ← by probability, not value based

    n-day run probability

    For a stock, compute how many N-day consecutive run in one direction → if I'm seeing A has 3-day ups now, this will tell me how likely it will have a 4-day up. Of course, if the probability is small, I should sell before its 4th day.

    This computation can be further broken down into:

    1. buy low sell high: probability when A fell, say 1%, how many days it took for it to recover 1% (now I have 1% gain)? 2%? and so on.

    2. buy high sell high: chasing an up wave. Compute n-day consecutive run probability.

    3. how long can it run in one direction?
    4. how much it can gain before losing steam ← quit at certain gain

    Now, what number used for this computation?

    • adj close price: volatile perhaps. All derived values are essentially trying to make this less volatile. But until I understand them better, I don't think they are relevant yet.

    a longer-term trend (NO)

    Apparently, daily volatility is too much. What if I combine different length of time frame, eg. 1-day AND 1-wk and 1-m? If all are in one direction, wouldn't it be a good indicator of the macro of this company?

    Not really. I don't see a basis for this theory, because I could equally argue why not using 2-wk, or 6-m data? If I don't do value research, just want to identify a value candidate, say A is on the up curve, why not use yr-to-yr growth of revenue?
